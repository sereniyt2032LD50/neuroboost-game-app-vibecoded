The Cognitive Science

Understand the neuro-scientific principles behind each training activity.

Memory Matrix & Visuospatial Memory

This activity is inspired by spatial recall tasks, such as the Corsi Block-Tapping Test. It targets the visuospatial sketchpad—the component of working memory responsible for temporarily storing and manipulating visual-spatial patterns.

Developing spatial memory enhances your ability to navigate environments, remember locations, and perform mental spatial rotations.

Stroop Reflex & Cognitive Control

Based on the classic Stroop Effect (first described by John Ridley Stroop in 1935). The task measures selective attention, cognitive flexibility, and response inhibition.

When spelling and font color mismatch, your brain experiences cognitive interference. Processing the word's meaning is automatic, while identifying the font color requires deliberate executive control. Training this helps you filter distractions and suppress impulsive reactions.

Schulte Grid & Peripheral Scanning

Originally designed as a diagnostic test for military pilot training, the Schulte Grid trains peripheral vision, sustained focus, and visual search speed.

By keeping your eyes fixed on the center of the grid and locating numbers sequentially using your peripheral scanning, you increase the field of visual search. This improves speed-reading skills and visual scanning efficiency.

Equation Rush & Working Memory

Arithmetic rush tasks stress rapid mathematical processing and cognitive gating—the ability to quickly load, manipulate, and update numerical representations in working memory while discarding old calculations.

Regular practice of speed calculations boosts mental processing speed, numerical literacy, and executive concentration under pressure.
